## The arc that produced this no_bet

This pick is the system's *first* public output, and it documents three
layers of methodology each doing their job — none of which would have
been visible without trying to actually publish.

### Layer 1: raw model — wildly overconfident on the home team

The uncalibrated logistic regression (v1.3.1 model, walk-forward CV at
baseline parity) saw a matchup where the away team has been 10-0 in
their last 10 games with a +24 net rating, and the home team is
6-4 with a +5 net rating. It produced **0.7943 probability for SAS to
win at home**.

That's not credible. The market priced the de-vigged fair probability
at 0.6225 — a 17.2pp disagreement with a sharp book on the most
liquid NBA market of the year. Either the model has discovered an
edge nobody else has, or the model is making a confident prediction
outside its calibrated range. The literature, the v1.3.x calibration
story in this repo's [`ENGINEERING_NOTES.md`](../docs/ENGINEERING_NOTES.md),
and basic priors all point to the second.

### Layer 2: isotonic calibration — overshot the other way

v1.4.0 commit 1 wrapped the model in `CalibratedClassifierCV(method=
'isotonic', cv=5)`. Internal 5-fold CV within each walk-forward
training set fits an isotonic regression on top of the base model's
outputs, so the stated probability matches the empirical frequency in
each bucket.

For this matchup, calibration pulled the prediction from **0.7943
down to 0.5095** — essentially a coin flip *from the model's
perspective*. The calibrator saw that "predictions in the [0.70, 0.80]
bucket actually win 68.5% of the time, not 75%" and applied that
correction. With this matchup's features sitting at the extreme of
the model's input distribution, the correction was aggressive.

This is itself a real finding: **the calibrator overshot in the
opposite direction.** Pre-calibration the model was 17pp above
market; post-calibration it's 11pp below market. The true probability
sits somewhere in between (probably close to the market's 0.62).
Isotonic with sparse tail data doesn't perfectly thread the needle —
it pulled too hard.

### Layer 3: disagreement guardrail — catches the residual unreliability

v1.4.0 commit 2 added a policy: if `|model_prob - fair_market_prob|`
exceeds 10 percentage points in either direction, auto-flag the pick
as `no_bet` regardless of what the EV math says. The math is built
on an unreliable probability when the model can't agree with a
sharp market.

The post-calibration disagreement is **11.30pp**, which still trips
the threshold. The pick lands as `no_bet`, reason
`"disagreement_too_large"`. No EV is reported, no Kelly fraction is
computed. The system explicitly refuses to recommend an action on
this matchup.

### What the system is actually saying

> "The model can't agree with Pinnacle on this game by more than 11
> points. Pinnacle has been doing this professionally for decades and
> the market is one of the most liquid in sports betting. The model
> is most likely wrong. Don't bet this."

That's exactly what a defensible production system should output here.

## Why we are publishing this `no_bet` rather than skipping it

Three reasons:

1. **Verifiability.** The git commit timestamp on
   [`1aae688472781f1a1aaf3efdb38e884b.json`](1aae688472781f1a1aaf3efdb38e884b.json)
   proves the system's decision for Game 1 existed before tipoff. The
   commit is part of the public record — anyone can verify with
   `git log --follow picks/1aae688472781f1a1aaf3efdb38e884b.json`.
   This is what starts the CLV track record, whether or not we bet.

2. **Methodology demonstration.** Most "pick services" only publish
   when they have a confident pick — they hide their no-bets, which
   means you can't see whether their system has discipline. Our first
   public output is a no_bet *because of an explicit, automated
   policy*. That's the artifact worth showing.

3. **It de-risks the next pick.** When the system DOES publish a
   `pick_side: home` or `pick_side: away` in a future game, the
   first thing a reviewer can verify is that the guardrail isn't
   defaulting everything to no_bet. Game 1's `no_bet` proves the
   guardrail works; future bets prove the system can actually fire
   when justified.

## CLV will still tell us what we learned

The closing line for Game 1 will be observable by tipoff. We'll
record it alongside this pick and compute the disagreement vs
*the closing line* (which is sharper than the opening line). Even
without a bet, this gives us our first data point on whether the
model's directional read (SAS slightly under-favored) tracks
market sharpening:

- If the line **moves toward NYK** (e.g., SAS -165 instead of -179),
  the market drifted closer to our model's view — a positive signal.
- If the line **moves toward SAS** (-185 or stronger), the market
  is doubling down on the home team and the model's calibration
  is even more out-of-step.

One data point is noise. But the record starts here.

## What's queued for v1.4.x

Surfaced by this specific result, in priority order:

1. **Better tail calibration.** Isotonic with internal 5-fold CV is
   overcorrecting on sparse extremes. Options to try: increase the
   internal CV folds (cv=10), try `method='sigmoid'` (Platt) which is
   more conservative on small samples, or use `cv='prefit'` with a
   dedicated held-out calibration set.
2. **Add bootstrap prediction intervals** so the JSON carries
   `model_prob_home_win_p5` and `_p95` alongside the point estimate.
   A "0.5095 (95% CI: 0.45-0.72)" reads much more honestly than
   "0.5095" alone.
3. **Add `closing_line_recorded_at` and `closing_line_*` fields** to
   the pick parquet so CLV can be computed automatically once a job
   captures the line ~5 minutes before tipoff. Currently this would
   be a manual edit.
4. **Confidence-based dynamic threshold.** A constant 10pp disagreement
   threshold is reasonable for unverified-edge models; once CLV
   shows positive results in specific regimes, the threshold can
   adapt. v1.5.x territory.
